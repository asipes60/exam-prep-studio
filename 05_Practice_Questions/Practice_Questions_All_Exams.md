## PART 1: CALIFORNIA LAW & ETHICS EXAM
*(Applies to LCSW, LMFT, and LPCC)*

---

**Q1 — Tarasoff / Duty to Warn**
A client tells you in session: "I am going to physically assault my former coworker when I see him at the gym next week." The coworker is identifiable. What is the therapist's legal duty under California law?

- A) Warn only the coworker
- B) Notify only law enforcement
- C) Warn the coworker and notify law enforcement within 24 hours
- D) Hospitalize the client immediately

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: California's Tarasoff law requires BOTH actions — warning the identifiable victim AND notifying law enforcement — within 24 hours of the threat. Notifying only one party does not fully discharge the duty. Hospitalization may or may not be warranted but is not the primary legal obligation here.*

---

**Q2 — Mandatory Reporting (Child Abuse)**
A 12-year-old client discloses that their stepfather hit them last week, leaving a bruise. The child's parent denies anything happened. What is the therapist required to do?

- A) Investigate the abuse before deciding whether to report
- B) Report only if the child corroborates the story in a second session
- C) Wait to see if abuse continues before reporting
- D) Make a phone report to CPS immediately and file a written report within 36 hours

**Answer: D**
*Rationale: "Reasonable suspicion" is the standard — a therapist does not need to confirm or investigate abuse before reporting. The phone report must be made immediately upon forming reasonable suspicion; the written report follows within 36 hours. Waiting or investigating violates mandated reporting law.*

---

**Q3 — Privilege vs. Confidentiality**
A court issues a subpoena for a client's therapy records. The client has not provided written authorization to release the records. The therapist cannot reach the client. What should the therapist do?

- A) Release the records — a court subpoena is sufficient authorization
- B) Release a summary but not the full record
- C) Do not release the records without a signed release or court order compelling disclosure
- D) Contact the client's attorney and release records to them

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: A subpoena alone is not sufficient authorization to release confidential records. The client holds the privilege, and without a signed release or a court order compelling disclosure, the therapist should not release the records. The appropriate response is to object through legal channels while attempting to reach the client.*

---

**Q4 — Minors and Consent**
A 14-year-old client has been receiving counseling with their own consent for 8 weeks. The parents call and request a copy of all session notes. How should the therapist respond?

- A) Provide the full records — parents are legal guardians
- B) Provide a summary only, omitting sensitive disclosures
- C) Decline — when a minor consents to their own treatment, the minor controls the privilege, not the parents
- D) Provide records only if the minor approves

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: Under California law, when a minor consents to their own treatment, the minor holds the privilege — not the parents. The therapist cannot release records to the parents without the minor's consent. Option D is partially correct in principle but framed imprecisely — C is the more complete and accurate answer.*

**Q5 — HIV Confidentiality**
A client discloses they are HIV positive and are having unprotected sex with a regular partner without the partner's knowledge of the HIV status. The client refuses to disclose. What is the therapist's obligation?

- A) Disclose to the partner — the risk of harm to a third party overrides confidentiality
- B) Report to public health authorities immediately
- C) Maintain confidentiality — HIV status cannot be disclosed without written client consent
- D) Only disclose if the partner is also a client of the same therapist

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: California law specifically protects HIV/AIDS status confidentiality. Unlike the Tarasoff duty to warn, there is no mandatory obligation to disclose HIV status to a partner. Disclosure requires written authorization from the client. State law takes precedence over ethical desire to protect third parties in this specific situation.*

**Q6 — HIPAA and Records Access**
A client requests a copy of their complete clinical file. They currently owe $800 in unpaid session fees. The therapist would like to withhold the records until the bill is paid. Under HIPAA, the therapist should:

- A) Withhold records until the balance is paid — providers may set this policy
- B) Provide records but charge a fee for copying
- C) Provide access to the records — fees owed cannot justify withholding records access
- D) Provide a summary in lieu of the full record

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: HIPAA does not permit withholding records access based on unpaid balances. The client has a right to access their health information. A reasonable copying fee may be charged, but access cannot be denied.*

**Q7 — Elder Abuse**
An 80-year-old client discloses that her adult son has been taking her Social Security checks without permission and has prevented her from seeing friends. As a therapist, you must:

- A) Discuss with the client whether they want you to report
- B) Encourage the client to report herself
- C) Report the suspected financial exploitation and isolation to APS immediately
- D) Contact the adult son for a collateral session before reporting

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: Social isolation and financial exploitation of an elder (65+) are forms of elder abuse that trigger mandatory reporting obligations. A therapist does not need to confirm or investigate — reasonable suspicion is sufficient. Waiting for client permission or doing an investigation first violates the mandatory reporting statute.*

**Q8 — Advertising and License Requirements**
A therapist is creating a new website for their private practice. Which of the following is required by California law?

- A) A photo of the therapist
- B) A list of all graduate school credentials
- C) The exact license title and license number
- D) A statement that fees are available upon request

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: California law requires that all advertising include the therapist's exact license title (e.g., "Licensed Clinical Social Worker" not just "therapist") and their license number. This allows clients and regulators to verify the practitioner's credentials.*

**Q9 — Referral Compensation**
A colleague offers to pay you $75 for every client you refer to their practice. Accepting this arrangement is:

- A) Ethical but not legal
- B) Legal but unethical
- C) Neither ethical nor legal — receiving compensation for referrals is both unethical and illegal in California
- D) Acceptable if documented in writing and disclosed to clients

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: Receiving compensation for client referrals (fee splitting) is prohibited by California law AND violates professional ethics codes. It is not saved by documentation or disclosure.*

**Q10 — 2024 Law Updates: Gravely Disabled**
California updated its definition of "gravely disabled" in 2024. Under the new definition, a person may be considered gravely disabled if they:

- A) Have refused all psychiatric medications for more than 30 days
- B) Have been homeless for more than 6 months
- C) Are unable to provide for their basic personal needs due to a mental health disorder, including individuals under a conservatorship
- D) Have been hospitalized more than twice in the past year

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: California expanded the "gravely disabled" definition in 2024 to include individuals who are the subject of conservatorship proceedings, recognizing that conservatorship-eligible individuals meet the threshold for involuntary treatment consideration. Options A, B, and D do not reflect the statutory definition.*

**Mini-Case 1: Adult Client, Mood Presentation**

*Client: 28-year-old male, referred by his primary care doctor. Reports "feeling numb" for the past 4 months, inability to feel pleasure in activities he used to enjoy (hiking, music), sleeping 10–12 hours daily but still exhausted, difficulty concentrating at work, and passive thoughts of "not wanting to be here anymore." Denies a specific plan or intent to act on suicidal thoughts. No prior psychiatric history. No substance use. No recent significant losses.*

**Q1.** During intake, which of the following questions would most directly help you assess immediate safety?
- A) "Can you tell me more about your relationship with your parents growing up?"
- B) "Have you ever tried medication for mood issues in the past?"
- C) "When you say you don't want to be here anymore, can you tell me more about those thoughts — do you have a plan or intent to act on them?"
- D) "How is your relationship with your primary care doctor?"

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: Assessing lethality requires clarifying the nature of suicidal ideation — passive vs. active, with or without plan/intent/means. Option C directly addresses this. The other options gather useful history but are not immediate safety priorities.*

---

**Q2.** Based on the intake, which diagnosis is MOST consistent with the clinical presentation?
- A) Persistent Depressive Disorder (PDD)
- B) Adjustment Disorder with Depressed Mood
- C) Major Depressive Disorder (MDD)
- D) Bipolar II Disorder — current episode depressed

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: The client meets criteria for MDD: anhedonia + depressed/numb mood for 4 months, hypersomnia, fatigue, concentration difficulties, passive SI — that's ≥5 symptoms for ≥2 weeks. There is no identifiable stressor to explain adjustment disorder, no history suggesting bipolar, and PDD requires 2 years duration.*

---

**Q3.** The MOST appropriate initial treatment approach for this client is:
- A) DBT skills training given his emotional numbing
- B) Motivational interviewing to explore readiness to engage in treatment
- C) CBT with behavioral activation to address anhedonia and increase engagement
- D) Psychodynamic therapy to explore unconscious conflicts

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: CBT with behavioral activation is a first-line, evidence-based treatment for MDD. Behavioral activation specifically targets anhedonia by gradually reintroducing rewarding activities. MI is not the primary intervention (client appears engaged). DBT is for emotional dysregulation/BPD. Psychodynamic therapy is an option but not the strongest first-line choice.*

**Mini-Case 2: Adolescent, Anxiety Presentation**

*Client: 16-year-old female. Parents bring her in because she has refused to attend school for 6 weeks. She reports intense fear of being called on in class, eating in the cafeteria, and talking to peers, worrying constantly that she will "do something embarrassing." She avoids social situations outside the home. She attends a small youth group at church without difficulty. No trauma history. Normal development. Academic performance was good prior to this school year.*

**Q4.** The most appropriate diagnosis for this client is:
- A) Generalized Anxiety Disorder
- B) Separation Anxiety Disorder
- C) Specific Phobia, situational type
- D) Social Anxiety Disorder (Social Phobia)

**Answer: D**
*Rationale: The client's fear is specifically in social/performance situations due to fear of embarrassment or negative evaluation by others — the defining feature of Social Anxiety Disorder. It is not a specific phobia (which is a specific object/situation unrelated to social evaluation). GAD involves pervasive worry across multiple domains. Separation anxiety involves fear of separation from attachment figures.*

---

**Q5.** The BEST evidence-based treatment for this client is:
- A) CBT with exposure therapy (graduated exposure to feared social situations)
- B) DBT skills group to improve interpersonal effectiveness
- C) Psychodynamic therapy to explore family-of-origin roots of social fear
- D) Supportive therapy with psychoeducation about anxiety

**Answer: A**
*Rationale: CBT with graduated exposure is the gold-standard evidence-based treatment for Social Anxiety Disorder. The exposure component is essential — avoidance maintains the anxiety. DBT is for BPD/emotional dysregulation. Psychodynamic and supportive approaches lack the same evidence base for specific anxiety disorders.*

**Q1 — Structural Family Therapy**
A family therapist notices that in sessions, the 14-year-old daughter consistently sides with her mother against the father during arguments, and the father has become increasingly distant. The therapist identifies this as:

- A) An example of positive triangulation
- B) An enmeshed boundary between the mother and daughter with a disengaged father
- C) A healthy coalition between parent and child
- D) A Bowenian triangle requiring differentiation work

**Answer: B**
*Rationale: In Structural Family Therapy, this pattern reflects a cross-generational coalition — the child is inappropriately aligned with one parent against the other, creating enmeshment between mother/daughter and disengagement with the father. While it involves triangulation (Bowen), the framing of subsystems, boundaries, and hierarchy is Minuchin/structural terminology.*

---

**Q2 — Gottman Method**
A couple reports that arguments always escalate when the husband raises concerns about household chores. The wife responds to his concerns with comments like "You're so lazy and irresponsible — I've always had to do everything." According to Gottman, the wife is engaging in:

- A) Stonewalling
- B) Defensiveness
- C) Criticism
- D) Contempt

**Answer: D**
*Rationale: Contempt involves attacking the partner's character or sense of self, often with a sense of superiority — "you're lazy and irresponsible" rather than addressing a specific behavior. Criticism attacks the person broadly ("you always...") but contempt goes further with moral judgment and superiority. The Gottman antidote to contempt is building a culture of appreciation.*

---

**Q3 — EFT for Couples**
An EFT therapist has helped a couple de-escalate their conflict cycle and has identified each partner's underlying attachment fears. The next stage of therapy involves:

- A) Teaching communication skills using the Speaker-Listener technique
- B) Exploring each partner's family of origin patterns
- C) Creating new emotional bonding experiences by helping each partner express their attachment needs and vulnerabilities
- D) Using cognitive restructuring to change negative attributions about the partner

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: Stage 2 of EFT is "Restructuring Attachment Bonds" — deepening engagement with partners' attachment needs and creating new bonding experiences. Communication skills (cognitive-behavioral) and family-of-origin exploration (Bowenian) are not EFT Stage 2 interventions. Cognitive restructuring is CBT.*

**Q4 — Crisis Management**
A client you've been seeing for 6 months calls between sessions and states: "I'm sitting with a bottle of pills. I'm tired and I don't want to do this anymore." What is the FIRST priority?

- A) Schedule an emergency session for the next day
- B) Assess lethality, express empathy, and determine safety plan
- C) Contact the client's family member as emergency contact
- D) Call 911 immediately

**Answer: B**
*Rationale: The first priority in a crisis is assessment — determine the seriousness of the risk before taking action. If lethality is high (specific plan, access to means, intent), calling 911 or contacting the family may become necessary. But intervening without assessment (calling 911 immediately for every crisis call) is not the first step. Express empathy first to maintain connection.*

**Q5 — Bowen Family Systems**
A therapist working with a 40-year-old client notices that whenever the client talks about their mother, they either idealize her completely or express intense rage, and they have cut off contact entirely after a recent argument. In Bowen's framework, this pattern most reflects:

- A) Triangulation
- B) Low differentiation of self with emotional cutoff
- C) Nuclear family emotional system — marital conflict
- D) Multigenerational transmission process

**Answer: B**
*Rationale: Emotional cutoff is Bowen's term for managing the intensity of unresolved family attachments by emotional and/or physical distancing. It reflects low differentiation — the client cannot maintain a connected, non-reactive stance with the parent. The all-or-nothing idealization/rage also reflects low differentiation (fusion then cutoff).*

**Q1 — Social Work Helping Process**
A social worker has just completed an initial assessment with a new client who is experiencing symptoms of depression and reports recent job loss. The social worker should:

- A) Immediately begin CBT to address cognitive distortions
- B) Refer the client to a psychiatrist for medication evaluation
- C) Work collaboratively with the client to develop a treatment plan with goals and interventions
- D) Psychoeducate the client about depression

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: After assessment comes planning — developing a collaborative treatment plan is the next step in the helping process. Immediate CBT or psychiatry referrals may eventually be appropriate but precede the planning stage. Psychoeducation is an intervention that belongs after planning.*

**Q2 — NASW Code of Ethics: Self-Determination**
A 72-year-old client with full decision-making capacity tells her social worker she has decided to stop treatment for her diabetes and is aware that her decision may shorten her life. The social worker disagrees strongly with this decision. The MOST appropriate response is:

- A) Consult with the client's physician to override the client's decision
- B) Explore the client's decision and ensure it is fully informed, then support her right to self-determination
- C) File a protective services report due to self-neglect
- D) Inform the client's family immediately so they can intervene

**Answer: B**
*Rationale: The NASW Code of Ethics strongly emphasizes client self-determination — competent adults have the right to make their own decisions, even ones the social worker disagrees with. The social worker's role is to ensure the decision is fully informed, not to override it. APS is for situations where the client lacks capacity or is being harmed by others — not voluntary medical decisions by a competent adult.*

**Q3 — DSM-5: Differential Diagnosis**
A 35-year-old client reports a 10-day period of decreased need for sleep (sleeping only 3 hours but feeling rested), increased goal-directed activity at work, unusual talkativeness, and making several large impulsive purchases. He states this is a positive change — "I finally feel good." He has also experienced two prior depressive episodes. What is the most likely diagnosis?

- A) Major Depressive Disorder with atypical features
- B) Bipolar II Disorder — current hypomanic episode
- C) Bipolar I Disorder — current manic episode
- D) Persistent Depressive Disorder with brief hypomanic periods

**Answer: B**
*Rationale: The client has a hypomanic episode (4+ days, elevated mood, decreased sleep, talkativeness, goal-directed activity, impulsivity) without hospitalization or severe impairment ("feels good"), plus prior depressive episodes = Bipolar II. Bipolar I requires a full manic episode (typically more severe, longer, or requiring hospitalization). 10 days of hypomania would meet the ≥4 day criterion.*

**Q4 — Group Therapy: Yalom**
In a process group, a member who previously identified as isolated and "unlike everyone else" begins to share their experience and discovers others in the group have had very similar struggles. According to Yalom, this therapeutic factor is called:

- A) Cohesiveness
- B) Altruism
- C) Universality
- D) Installation of hope

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: Universality is the curative factor where group members realize they are not alone in their struggles — others have similar experiences, thoughts, and feelings. This is especially powerful for clients who had felt uniquely burdened or shameful.*

**Q5 — Ethics: Dual Relationships**
A social worker is assigned to provide therapy to a client and discovers, during the first session, that the client is a close friend of the social worker's spouse. The social worker should:

- A) Continue treatment — the connection is indirect and likely won't affect the therapeutic relationship
- B) Discuss the situation with a supervisor and document the conversation
- C) Refer the client to another provider to avoid the potential dual relationship complications
- D) Tell the client about the connection and allow the client to decide whether to continue

**Answer: C**
*Rationale: The NASW Code of Ethics directs social workers to avoid dual relationships that could impair professional judgment or harm the client. A close connection to the client through the social worker's spouse creates enough relational complexity to warrant referral. While consultation (B) has value, it doesn't resolve the dual relationship issue. Client decision-making (D) doesn't resolve the ethical problem.*

## ADDITIONAL QUICK-FIRE QUESTIONS

**Q:** What is the defining feature of Borderline Personality Disorder vs. Bipolar Disorder in terms of symptom pattern?
**A:** BPD mood changes are triggered by interpersonal events and last hours; bipolar mood episodes last days to weeks and are not necessarily triggered by interpersonal events.

**Q:** What is the antidote to "stonewalling" in the Gottman model?
**A:** Physiological self-soothing — taking a break to calm down before continuing the conversation.

**Q:** Under the NASW Code of Ethics, can a social worker enter a sexual relationship with a former client after treatment ends?
**A:** The NASW Code of Ethics prohibits sexual relationships with former clients, regardless of the time elapsed since termination ended. This is an absolute prohibition.

**Q:** What is the key distinction between Acute Stress Disorder and PTSD?
**A:** Duration — Acute Stress Disorder is 3 days to 1 month after the trauma; PTSD is more than 1 month after.

**Q:** In motivational interviewing, what is the appropriate response when a client resists change?
**A:** "Roll with resistance" (now called "rolling with discord") — do not argue or confront; reflect ambivalence and emphasize autonomy.

**Q:** A therapist suspects a child client is being sexually abused. What is the standard of evidence required to make a mandated report?
**A:** "Reasonable suspicion" — not certainty, not confirmed evidence. If a similarly trained professional would suspect abuse based on the same information, that is sufficient.
