# California BBS Law & Ethics Exam — Complete Study Guide
*Applies to: LCSW, LMFT, and LPCC candidates | Updated 2025*

---

## EXAM OVERVIEW

The California Board of Behavioral Sciences (BBS) requires all associates (ASW, AMFT, APCC) to pass a Law and Ethics Exam before receiving their license. There is a **separate exam for each license type**, though the content overlaps significantly.

| Feature | Detail |
|---|---|
| Total questions | 75 multiple-choice |
| Scored questions | 50 |
| Unscored (pretest) | 25 |
| Time allowed | 90 minutes (~1.2 min/question) |
| Passing score | ~35/50 (70%) — varies slightly by exam version |
| Testing vendor | Pearson VUE |
| Retake wait period | 90 days |
| Additional req. for retake | 12 hours of extra law & ethics coursework |
| Results | Typically within 24–48 hours of testing |

**Content split:** Approximately 40% law / 60% ethics

Once passed, **you do not need to retake** the California Law and Ethics Exam for licensure.

---

## WHEN TO TAKE IT

- **LPCC (APCC):** Must be taken within the **first year of APCC registration**. If not passed, must continue attempting at least once per registration period until passed.
- **LMFT / LCSW:** Must pass the Law & Ethics exam **before** you can apply to take the clinical exam.

## EXAM CONTENT DOMAINS

The BBS develops the exam around **147 areas of knowledge**. The content is divided between legal requirements and professional ethics. Key domains include:

### 1. Confidentiality & Privilege
- Difference between **confidentiality** (clinical duty) and **privilege** (legal right in court proceedings)
- Limits of confidentiality — when disclosure is permitted or required
- Confidentiality in group/couples/family therapy
- Confidentiality after client death
- Psychotherapy notes vs. general clinical records (HIPAA distinction)
- **HIV confidentiality:** California law mandates protecting a client's HIV status unless the client provides written permission — state law prevails over ethical standards

### 2. Mandated Reporting
- **Child abuse:** Must report "reasonable suspicion" — phone report immediately, written report within **36 hours**
- Minors don't need to be physically present in California for a report to be required; report to CA CPS
- **Elder/dependent adult abuse:** Must report for individuals age 65+ or dependent adults; includes social isolation
- **NOT mandated to report:** Domestic violence (between adults), past abuse disclosed by an adult who was abused as a child (no current minor at risk)
- Consensual sex between 14–17 year olds is not a mandatory report; however, a 16-year-old with a 21-year-old IS a mandatory report
- "Reasonable suspicion" means another similarly-trained therapist would also suspect abuse from the same information

### 3. Tarasoff / Duty to Warn & Protect
- Applies when a client expresses a **serious, credible threat of physical violence** against an **identifiable victim**
- California law requires: **warn the intended victim AND report to law enforcement within 24 hours**
- Does NOT apply to threats of property damage (e.g., vandalism) — must be physical harm
- Threat dependent on another's death (e.g., "if my mother dies, I'll...") lacks **imminence** — no Tarasoff duty triggered
- Key issue: **imminence + identifiable victim + physical harm**

### 4. Minors & Consent
- Minors may consent to their own treatment at age **12+** if parental involvement is not in their best interest
- If a minor consents to their own treatment, they are **financially responsible** for fees
- Before involving parents, therapist should **first determine how the client feels** about parental involvement
- Minors who consent to their own treatment have privilege rights (not parents)

### 5. HIPAA
- Know the difference between **psychotherapy notes** (highest protection) and the **general clinical record**
- Patients have the right to access their records; cannot be withheld solely due to unpaid fees
- Provider can prepare a **summary** (must be available within 10 working days)
- All authorizations to release information must include an **end date**
- Providers taking third-party reimbursement must understand and comply with HIPAA
- **Notice of Privacy Practices** must be provided at the start of treatment
- Incidental uses and disclosures are permitted with "reasonable safeguards" in place

### 6. Professional Competence & Scope of Practice
- Therapists must work within their competence areas
- Required to refer when a client's needs exceed your scope
- Advertising must include exact license title and license number
- Receiving compensation for client referrals is **both unethical AND illegal**
- Interstate compact rules for telehealth practice (LCSW Compact, Counseling Compact)

### 7. Subpoenas & Legal Issues
- When records are subpoenaed without a release and you cannot reach the client, **do not release records without a court order or signed release**
- For minors, identify the **guardian ad litem** assigned by the court to decide about privilege
- Psychotherapy privilege can be waived by the client (or their representative)

### 8. Involuntary Hospitalization & Gravely Disabled
- Clients who are "gravely disabled" (unable to provide for basic needs due to mental disorder) can be held on a 5150
- Updated 2024 California definition of "gravely disabled" expanded to include individuals in conservatorships
- Suicidal thoughts alone may not warrant hospitalization — need **active intent + specific plan**

### 9. Suicide & Legal Duty
- Therapists do **not** have a **legal** duty to prevent suicide
- However, a clinician can be found **legally negligent** if their treatment plan overlooks documented suicidal tendencies
- Ethical duty requires assessment of risk and appropriate clinical response

### 10. Ethics Codes by License Type
- **LCSW:** NASW Code of Ethics (primary)
- **LMFT:** CAMFT Code of Ethics and AAMFT Code of Ethics
- **LPCC:** ACA Code of Ethics

### 11. Business & Administrative Requirements
- Informed consent requirements at the start of treatment
- Telehealth rules and consent requirements
- No Surprises Act — good faith cost estimates for uninsured/self-pay clients
- Record retention requirements (California = 7 years post-termination; minors = 7 years post-18th birthday)
- Supervision requirements for associates

## 2024–2025 UPDATES TO KNOW

The 2024 BBS Exam Plan introduced several new and updated content areas:

- **Expanded definition of "gravely disabled"** — includes individuals in conservatorship proceedings
- **Changes in elder and dependent adult abuse reporting protocols**
- **Changes in privilege law** — updated guidance on when privilege applies
- **Interstate compacts** — both the Counseling Compact (LPCC) and LCSW Compact allow multistate practice
- **Ethical use of AI** in therapeutic settings
- **CARE Courts** — California's new court-ordered treatment process for severely mentally ill individuals
- **Mental Health Access Improvement Act** — expanded Medicare reimbursement for LMFTs and LCSWs

## SAMPLE PRACTICE QUESTIONS

**Q1.** A client tells you they plan to physically harm a coworker next Friday. What is your Tarasoff duty?
- A) Warn only the coworker
- B) Call the police only
- **C) Warn the coworker AND notify police within 24 hours** ✓
- D) Hospitalize the client immediately

*Rationale: California law requires both actions — warning the identifiable victim AND notifying law enforcement within 24 hours.*

---

**Q2.** A 15-year-old client who previously consented to their own treatment asks you not to tell their parents about the sessions. Who controls the privilege for these records?
- A) The parents, as legal guardians
- B) The therapist
- **C) The minor client** ✓
- D) The court

*Rationale: When a minor consents to their own treatment, they hold the privilege — not their parents.*

---

**Q3.** An adult client discloses that they were physically abused by a parent throughout childhood. The abuse ended when they turned 18 and they now have no contact with the parent. Are you mandated to report?
- **A) No — the client is an adult and there is no current minor at risk** ✓
- B) Yes — all disclosures of child abuse must be reported
- C) Only if the client gives permission
- D) Yes, but only within 36 hours

*Rationale: Mandated reporting applies to current abuse or reasonable suspicion of ongoing risk. Past abuse with no current minor at risk does not create a mandatory report.*

---

**Q4.** Your client discloses they are HIV positive and plans to have unprotected sex with their partner without telling them. Can you break confidentiality?
- A) Yes — you are mandated to warn the partner
- **B) Only with written permission from the client** ✓
- C) Yes — the risk of harm to another outweighs confidentiality
- D) No — you must maintain confidentiality absolutely

*Rationale: California law specifically protects HIV status confidentiality. Unlike Tarasoff situations, HIV disclosure requires written client consent — state law takes precedence.*

---

**Q5.** A client owes $500 in unpaid fees and requests copies of their records. Under HIPAA, you should:
- A) Withhold records until the balance is paid
- B) Release only a summary, not the full record
- **C) Allow access — records cannot be withheld due to unpaid fees** ✓
- D) Request a court order before releasing

*Rationale: HIPAA prohibits withholding records based solely on unpaid fees.*

## STUDY STRATEGIES & TIPS

### Approach to Answering Questions
- Most questions are scenario-based; identify the **most ethical AND most legal** answer
- When law and ethics conflict, **state law takes precedence** over ethical codes in California
- When both apply, choose the **most protective** option for the client
- Questions about what to do "first" — generally assess before acting; contact the client before third parties

### Key Distinctions to Know Cold
- Confidentiality vs. Privilege
- Mandated reporting (required) vs. permissive reporting (allowed)
- Psychotherapy notes vs. clinical record
- Legal duty vs. ethical duty
- State law vs. federal law (state law often more protective in CA)

### Common Traps
- "Should" vs. "Must" — the exam distinguishes between ethical best practices and legal requirements
- Telehealth consent is separate from general informed consent
- ESL accommodations: submit through the BBS; disability accommodations: submit through Pearson VUE
